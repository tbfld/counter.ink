The French publisher [Minuit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Les_%C3%89ditions_de_Minuit) just put out [Jean Echenoz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Echenoz)’s latest book, *[Bristol](https://isbn.nu/9782707355928)*, and I wrote this in a comment on another platform:

> Echenoz isn’t very well known in anglophonia, which is a pity because — from what I know of his work at least — it’s great. From my days in publishing I have the uncorrected proofs of an English translation of his novel *Lac*, which I don’t think was ever published, not sure why.

...followed by...

> Ohhhh, I see...it took 20 years and a name change: *Lac* was translated as *[Chopin’s Move](https://search.worldcat.org/title/54022621)* ([Dalkey Archive](https://www.dalkeyarchive.com/), 2004). Or something like that, a bit hard to tell. No surprise that Dalkey would do it: they’re the inheritor of [New Directions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Directions_Publishing) and [Barney Rosset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barney_Rosset)’s [Grove Press](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grove_Press) in their best days, the two publishers that pretty much defined what Americans think modern literature is (or at least *thought* what it is 🫤). Dalkey got Echenoz’s book from the publisher [Matthew Godine](https://godine.com/pages/about), who issued the uncorrected proofs that I have. I know I got them from the [New Press](https://thenewpress.com/) in its early days, because [we published several of Echenoz’s novels](https://thenewpress.com/authors/jean-echenoz). (NP was founded by [André Schiffrin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Schiffrin) after Random House fired him from Pantheon, the imprint his father founded. [André’s father Jacques](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Schiffrin) had founded [Pléiade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biblioth%C3%A8que_de_la_Pl%C3%A9iade) in Paris.) I’d guess that [Juan Garcia de Oteyza](https://aperture.org/editorial/juan-garcia-de-oteyza-1962-2013/) acquired *Lac/Chopin* for Godine when he worked with them on his Eridanos imprint[^1] (largely funded by [Bompiani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bompiani)), which issued *dazzlingly* beautiful editions of lesser-known Latin American writers — flawless, every one of them, in their curation, translation, design, and production. I worked with Juan at the US office of the Italian publisher [Marsilio](https://www.marsilioeditori.it/marsilio-editori-about-us), which was run by Umberto Eco’s son, so there was an endless parade of literati coming through its doors. Juan went on to become Mexico’s cultural attaché to the US and to head up the [Aperture Foundation](https://aperture.org/), before dying way too young. He was — truly — one of the greatest people I’ve ever know, incredibly cultured and salt-of-the-earth in equal measure. It’s said that a person’s memory dies the last time someone mentions them, so I will *never* miss a chance to remember Juan in public. 🙏🏼 
> 
> I’m conjuring up all these details not to drop names but to leave breadcrumb trails for anyone interested in these entangled literary histories — especially Juan’s Eridanos imprint.
